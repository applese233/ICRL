<information>
Marie Curie was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize. She won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1903, sharing it with her husband Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel for their joint research on the radiation phenomena discovered by Becquerel. In 1911, Curie won her second Nobel Prize, this time in Chemistry, for her discovery of the elements radium and polonium. She was the first person ever to win Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields, and remains the only woman to have won in two different scientific fields.

<information>
Tungsten (symbol W, atomic number 74) has one of the highest boiling points of all elements. The boiling point of tungsten is 5,555°C (10,031°F). This makes it useful for applications requiring materials with extremely high melting and boiling points, such as in light bulb filaments, electron tubes, and spacecraft components. Tungsten also has the highest melting point of all pure elements at 3,422°C (6,192°F).
